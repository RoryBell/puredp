A Guide to Street Fighter 6

Welcome!

This guide provides the fundamentals of fighting games. You'll be able to apply what you learn here to quickly pick up any other fighting game. Even the mechanics that seem specific to Street Fighter 6 often have an equivalent in other games, serving similar purposes.

This guide is designed with your progression in mind, so you shouldn't need to skip ahead. As long as you take the time to implement each element as it is introduced, the next one should be the most relevant to your current needs. You can skip ahead to see what's coming, but I don't recommend skipping the implementation steps.

While this will take genuine effort, I believe this structure will give you a massive head start and prepare you to experience what fighting games have to offer much sooner.

As you progress, you'll experience a cycle of rapid improvement followed by lengthy plateaus. This is a universal experience. It simply means you need to add another tool to your kit to see improvement again. The idea that you can finish learning a fighting game is madnessâ€”there is always more knowledge to strive for.

Pure Defensive Play (PDP)

  • Never jump.
  • Block low unless they jump or if you recognize an overhead attack.
  • Only move your character with the intention of getting into the correct range so you're always ready to anti air a jump in.
  • While the opponent is grounded, only attack if you are guaranteed a punish counter.
  • When the opponent jumps into your space, counter with an anti air (2HP).
  • Throw when the opponent is in range.
  • DI is exclusively a counter measure. Never use it offensively or on prediction.

While PDP will often lead to dull stalemate scenarios, the purpose is not to win. It's to build knowledge of the game's characters so you can identify punish windows, deploy the correct defensive options, and to learn how to recognize player patterns. In any well made fighting games, every action has an intended counter. If you can work out what your options are for most scenarios, your defense will become nearly unbreakable.
The secondary purpose of PDP is to act as an introduction to neutral and footsies via your anti air spacing.

PDP is entirely about observation and reaction. While you will naturally start to see patterns and begin predicting the opponent's moves, acting on prediction is not a part of the gameplan. Only react.

Don't pay attention to health bars. The points don't matter. Don't allow your decisions to be affected by the health bars.

If the opponent gains a life lead and goes full screen for the timeout, let them. Use the time to practice specials/supers. Remember: you are there for your own development, not the rank points. The points don't matter.
With this mentality, you will actually climb the ranks faster as you will improve enough to keep the points you earn.

Try to avoid randomly pressing buttons in the hope something will work. Out random-ing the opponent isn't progress you can take ownership of.

PDP has no way to counter grapplers. This is because grapplers require prediction to beat. As prediction is banned...grapplers will beat you!

When you feel comfortable with all the elements of this plan, pick a modifier to introduce to your gameplan. Only choose 1 at a time so you can focus on integrating the modifier fully.

In lower ranks, many players tend to rush in and mindlessly press buttons, hoping something will connect. To counter this, patiently wait for them to perform an action that leaves them exposed long enough for you to hit them before they recover. This is known as a Punish Counter.

In the early stages, focus on punishing your opponent's uppercut or super moves. These are easily recognizable and reactable.

You will initially gravitate towards throws as your go to punish move. While this is a reliable option as it's fast, you can do better. Using a normal offers up combo potential. Simple combos can do the same amount of damage as a throw, but a full combo can easily do 5 times the damage!

As you should be blocking low the majority of the time, you will block a lot of sweep attempts. These can be punished with a sweep of your own.

If you are trying to learn punish windows without the help of frame data, limit how many moves you try to challenge. I'd suggest identifying one move the opponent likes to use and trying a light normal after a block. If you see "punish counter", you are on the right track.

If you press a button after every opponent attack, you will almost certainly lose the interaction and you will rarely learn from it.

Mastering your awareness and management of the distance between you and your opponent is vital for any gameplan.

Right now, your goal is to keep the opponent's potential forward jump arc directly infront of you.

If they jump when you aren't spaced correctly, attempting to anti air will likely result in you getting punished.

Don't treat anti airing as a generalised response to the opponent being airborne. Positioning is everything.

Often, when you try to increase the distance, they will chase you down. Trying to outrun them is usually futile, as you walk forward faster than you walk back, and they will catch up.

Your best answer to this is to crouch block and get ready for their attacks. If you are feeling brave, you can use a fast normal (I recommend 2MP) to punish their walk in.

Use combos for punish counter extensions. Capitalising on punish counters is key to winning a round. Start with cancelling your punish counter normal into a fireball. As you get more comfortable, increase the complexity. Here are demonstrations of some combos. There are countless more you could learn, but these will help you get started.

When practicing combos in training mode, take it slow at first. Is there a particular or link or cancel that is causing you trouble? Just do that one part until you have it down, then retry the full combo.

You can literally slow it down by dropping the game speed to 50% in the training mode settings. Use this. It's very useful to understand the timing of your inputs as this game can move too fast for you to see what went wrong while at full speed.

Keep the input display on while practicing to ensure your inputs are precise. This helps to prevent the input system from misinterpreting your commands and triggering unexpected moves. If you catch yourself mashing, slow down and execute the moves correctly. Slowly building up the muscle memory is key.

Before bringing a combo to a match, make sure you can execute it perfectly in training mode first. Once you can do the combo 10 times in a row in both directions, introduce it into your gameplan.

You will drop combos when you first try them in a match. The pressure of a real game is a real problem. Ideally you want to get your execution of a combo to a level where you can do it on autopilot. Then the pressure won't affect your execution.

Move on to the next modifier when you can do the basic combos, the lvl1 combo and the lvl3 combo consistently in a match.

Before starting this section. Make sure that your anti air game is solid. There are two main checks you need to pass before continuing.

  • Can you land anti airs consistently?
  • Do you only attempt to anti air when you are in the correct range?

    • If you don't pass these checks, this section will provide a complication that you aren't ready for.

    If you have passed these checks, it's time to replace 2HP with DP for anti airing.

    From the start, you were told to use 2HP to anti air. This isn't a bad option as it's a very simple input that provides a small amount of air invincibility and a suitably placed hitbox with decent damage.

    (Light green is a regular hurtbox, dark green is an air invincible hurtbox, red is a hitbox)

    You have a better option.

    Light DP instantly provides total air invincibility from frame 1. The startup is nearly halved, the hitbox is larger and more consistently placed throughout the move while also staying active for a bit longer, and the damage is increased.

    This is a significant upgrade. While it is more difficult to perform, with practise the difference will be negligible. Because it's air invincible from frame 1, it actually gives you more time to execute while eliminating concerns of getting counter hit.

    Move on when you can instinctually DP and your anti airing is consistently better than when you used 2HP

    Use parry when the opponent does a fireball, slow special, a DI when your health is too low to absorb it, or a super. For now, parry is to be used as a reaction block, not a pre-emptive one. This should be your third modifier.

    Parry has the same block stun as if you did a regular block. The benefits are not losing drive guage (you actually build drive guage when parrying hits), no wallsplat if you absorb a DI, and a chance at getting a perfect parry. When you perfect parry (it's a 2 frame window, so very difficult to do deliberately), you reset to neutral immediately but any damage you do as a punish counter starts with a 50% penalty. A reasonable trade off for stealing a turn.

    The main reason it's so important to only parry on reaction is because if you get thrown while in the parry state, you take +70% damage when compared to a normal throw. This happens because throwing a parry gets treated as a punish counter.

    The recovery frames for a parry that isn't in blockstun add up to just over half a second. In Street Fighter, half a second feels like forever.

    If you have committed to a parry but think you're about to be thrown, your best option is to drive rush out of the parry. This costs very little drive meter and you don't have to take the full punish counter throw.

    Learn how to block while doing a delayed throw tech to cover a 50/50 mixup. It covers you from getting struck by a meaty, and being thrown. This can be achieved in a single training session using the premade throwing scenario in training mode. Once you know, you know.

    Most moves in fighting games are too fast to be reacted to by a human, so a key component of high level play is making educated guesses as to what your opponent's going to do next.

    As your knowledge and reaction times improve, you will find yourself in situations where you are confident you know what the opponents next move will be. Make note of when these moments occur, and how often you were right. When your predictions consistently come true, ease up on the no prediction rule. Strike first! (though prediction DI will almost always be a bad idea. Don't start that habit)

    Whereas online fighting games used to be 1 and done, meaning you had to come up with generalised tactics and adapt quickly, ranked matches in street fighter 6 are best of 3. The purpose of your first game should be to collect data on the opponent so even if you lose, you have 2 more games to get the overall victory.

    When collecting data, you need to make a mental note of how the opponent acts during common situations and what offensive tactics they try to use. Use this data to put their tactics higher up in your mental stack so you can respond faster when these situations next occur.

    A common scenario where prediction is necessary is on wakeup. Regardless of which side of the scenario you are on, you will need to make a guess as to what the opponent will do next. Delay Tech was an introduction to this concept, but it can get far more complicated.

    By now you will have seen opponents wake up super or wake up OD DP. Against a defensive player, such as yourself, this has proven futile. Though it may surprise you to know that it can be a viable tactic if you correctly guess the standing player will attempt to attack you on your wakeup. Make sure you know what you are doing when this situation arises. The punish you receive for getting it wrong will be devastating. Not just for your health bar, but your drive/super meter as well.

    This is where the gameplan starts to become unrecognisable from how it was originally. It's time to go on the offensive!

    Any cancellable normals can make use of a DRC to extend combos at the cost of 3 bars. You have two choices of inputs for a DRC, dash or MP+MK. For now Drive Rush is only to be used for combo extensions.

    The move you DRC into gains additional frame advantage (the opponent will be in Hitstun for longer) which opens up new combo routes

    Here's a simple but high damage punish combo to get you started:

    It's important to be constantly aware of your drive guage. This will inform you of your options when starting a combo.

    A good general rule to follow: only put yourself in burnout if you think it'll get you the win.

    Long Drive Rush combos highlight how aggressive the damage scaling can be. It's impossible to give a succinct guide on damage scaling, so you will need to explore this in training mode or read move descriptions in FAT to discover the scaling implications of every action.
    Though it's worth pointing out that it's still optimal to use supers at the end of long combos as their scaling floor is much higher than other moves (lvl1 30%, lvl2 40%, lvl3 50%) which means even if you do a really long combo that brings the damage of each subsequent hit down to sub 30%, finishing with a super can still deal significant damage.

    It's time to break the most important rule.

    You were strictly forbidden from jumping in the gameplan, and for good reason. New players quickly form the idea that jumping is a good confusion tactic. This couldn't be further from the truth as you commit to a 3/4 of a second jump arc. This particular bad habit doesn't leave some people. You occasionally see master ranked players non stop jumping when they panic. Not because it's a good tactic, but because they struggle to unlearn it. It only worked in the lower ranks where anti air tactics aren't common.

    By following the anti airing element of the gameplan, you have learned that if you jump into the opponent's space in neutral, you're giving them the turn.

    As a grounded player that has made it this far in the guide, you should find all your actions are deliberate choices. Slowly introducing jumps into your gameplan will open up new gameplay opportunities.

  • You can finally play against grapplers! the defining part of a grapplers gameplan is to get within throw range and force the opponent to guess if they will strike or command throw (a 50/50 mixup). jumping means you have the final tool to deal with both options.
  • You now have a way to deal with an opponent's fireballs when you are in burn out. Be mindful of your spacing and timing. With all your anti airing practise, I am trusting you to avoid fireballs safely.
  • You can now punish opponents who do unsafe actions while in jump range. eg. if you correctly predict the opponent will throw a fireball while you are in jump range, you can hit them with a forward jump attack to start a punish combo.
  • Combo extension. You will have noticed that when you get stunned, the opponent usually starts their next combo with a jump in attack. jump in attacks as a combo starter is often the way to start your highest damage combos. this is because they don't incur initial damage scaling and allow you to follow up with another heavy normal as the second hit.

    • If you start mindlessly jumping, your Street Fighter license will be revoked.

    Developing a decent fireball game is vital. You will need to spend time learning exactly how your character's fireball options work. Primarily you will need to know, per button, how fast they are and how many hits they have

    Challenging an opponents fireball game is a very fast session of rock paper scissors where you need to be mindful of their options as well as yours.

    The main things to consider:

  • Do they have the drive meter to do an OD fireball? If you throw out a regular fireball, the opponent's OD fireball will win and hit you.
  • Are they in jump in range? If you throw out a fireball and they jump, they might hit you before you recover.
  • Does the opponent's character have projectile invincible moves? This could be used to bypass your fireball and hit you. If they have a super ready, it has the potential to be devastating. You will often see Cammy's using this option to hit you with their lvl3.

    • All lvl1 supers are vulnerable to projectiles, so if you knock an opponent down, timing a fireball to be over them on wakeup while they only have lvl1 is a safe way to limit their options.

    Avoid mindlessly throwing fireballs. It should be an informed choice.

  • Parry: Throwing is your best answer. It's considered a punish counter so you will get more damage from a throw if you bait a parry first.
  • Rush: Players often abuse this to attempt skipping neutral. Simply use a light normal to stop them.
  • Reversal: This move can be executed during block stun or upon wakeup by inputting 6HPHK. It's entirely invincible but inflicts only grey damage (recoverable unless you're hit, in which case it adds to the incoming damage). Your best strategy is to block the Drive Reversal, which leaves you at +6 frame advantage, allowing you to counter with any attack that has a 5-frame startup or less.
  • Impact: This requires the most situational awareness to respond to. If you are cornered, you are at risk of wall splat. If you are cornered and burned out, you are at risk of getting stunned. Your options are:
    • Counter Drive Impact. Make sure you have enough health to absorb the initial hit
    • Parry. Unlike regular blocking, you won't get wall splat if you parry. you also have a chance to perfect parry to steal the turn
    • Throw
    • Armour Break either manually by doing a fast 3 hit combo, or by using an armour breaking move such as a super
  • Watch your replays. There is no better way to work out where you can improve than by watching your gameplay without the responsibility of execution. Make notes on the good and the bad so you can build an understanding of your habits.
    Play along with the replay in your mind. See if your decision making is any different when you don't feel pressured.
  • Learn the rest of your main character's moves. This will be the most time intensive task, but slowly building a full understanding of your character's toolkit will open up punish and combo opportunities. Integrating the moves one at a time will enable you to understand the intended use case for each move.
  • Drive Rush Counter. When the opponent tries to drive rush, use a light normal to hit them first.
  • Advanced spacing. When you start to understand the character's maximum strike range, adjust the movement rule in the gameplan so you are always JUST outside of your opponent's strike range.
  • Whiff punish. When you can consistently stay just outside of the opponent's strike range, react to their button with a button of your own to punish.
  • Use of a fast normal when the opponent tries to walk into your space (normally a crouching medium attack).

    • When you are able to use this gameplan to consistently win the interactions it's intended for, you can start to relax the rules begin creating gameplans of your own. This is where fighting games really begin!

    HA! Got you! This is the frame data section!

    For your current needs, knowledge of frame data serves two main purposes

    • Punish Counters. If an opponent's move whiffs, you can see how long you have to get in with an attack. Or if you block an attack, you can know if you get out of Blockstun before they finish their recovery, providing a window to start your turn.
    • Combos. Having a database of the startup, cancel types (can it cancel into a special? super?), and Hitstun for all moves trivialises building combos.
      Choosing a move to initiate an attack can be greatly informed by the combo routes you learn to maximise your damage output. Optimising for the most common scenarios means you will need to win fewer interactions to win a round.

    If you want to improve your knowledge fast, the best way is to utilise frame data tools. The barrier to entry is so much lower than it may seem.

    Keeping a log of moves you struggled to deal with can prove invaluable. Pairing this log with a frame data tool such as Ultimate Frame Data, which has a gif of each move, along with visualised hitboxes and frame data, to help you identify a way to counter a particular move. This eliminates the much slower trial and error phase.

    Frame data may seem a needlessly nerdy rabbit hole, but if used correctly, your time spent accessing frame data tools is very brief. You have a specific question you want answered, go in, get the answer, get out. The upfront intimidation from all the information will fall away when you realise how much faster it makes learning character matchups and your own character.

    Trying to store all of the data in your head is a waste of time. Use frame data tools to answer specific questions as they arise, and over time you will build an intuitive understanding of all character's strengths and weaknesses
